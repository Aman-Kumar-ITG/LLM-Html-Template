Foundations of LLMs

Large Language Models are probabilistic machines that predict the next token in a sequence. Everything they do — writing code, answering questions, translating languages — emerges from this single capability, scaled across billions of parameters and trillions of training tokens.

Transformers, Tokens, and Embeddings

Tokenization

Before any processing, raw text is split into tokens — sub-word units. Most modern LLMs use Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) or SentencePiece. The word "unhappiness" might become ["un", "happiness"] or ["un", "happ", "iness"] depending on the tokenizer's vocabulary. GPT-4's tokenizer has ~100k tokens. A useful rule of thumb: 1 token ≈ ¾ of a word in English.

Embeddings

Each token maps to a high-dimensional vector (e.g., 4096 dimensions for a 7B parameter model). These vectors are learned during training and encode semantic relationships. Crucially, positional embeddings are added so the model knows where each token sits in the sequence — transformers have no inherent sense of order without this.

Self-Attention: The Core Mechanism

Self-attention is what makes transformers powerful. For each token, it computes three vectors:

Query (Q): "What am I looking for?" Key (K): "What do I contain?" Value (V): "What information do I carry?"

The attention score between token i and token j is the dot product of Qi and Kj, scaled by √d to prevent gradient explosion. This score determines how much token i should "attend to" (draw information from) token j.

Analogy: The Cocktail Party

Imagine you're at a loud party (all the tokens). You ask a question (Query). Everyone holds up a name tag describing themselves (Key). You decide who to listen to based on how relevant their name tag is to your question. Then you absorb what they're saying (Value). Multi-head attention means you can simultaneously listen for different types of information — one head tracks grammar, another tracks semantics, another tracks co-reference.

Why Multi-Head?

Instead of one attention computation, the model runs 32–128 parallel attention "heads," each with its own Q/K/V projection matrices. Different heads learn to capture different relationships: syntactic dependencies, co-reference, semantic similarity, positional patterns. The outputs are concatenated and linearly projected back to the model dimension.

Training Phases in Detail

Pre-training: The model sees trillions of tokens and learns to predict the next token. This is unsupervised — no labeled data needed. The loss function is cross-entropy between the predicted token distribution and the actual next token. This phase accounts for ~95% of total compute.

Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT): The pre-trained model is fine-tuned on curated (prompt, ideal_response) pairs. This teaches it to follow instructions and produce helpful outputs instead of just completing text.

RLHF / DPO: Human preference data (which response is better?) trains a reward model, which then guides further optimization. DPO (Direct Preference Optimization) achieves similar results without a separate reward model by directly optimizing the policy from preference pairs.

Inference Optimization

KV Cache: During autoregressive generation, the model caches the Key and Value tensors from previous tokens so they don't need recomputation. For a 70B model with 128K context, the KV cache alone can consume 40+ GB of GPU memory.

Speculative Decoding: A smaller "draft" model generates candidate tokens quickly, and the larger model verifies them in parallel — accepting correct guesses and fixing wrong ones. This can yield 2–3× speedup without quality loss.

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)

RAG solves the fundamental tension: LLMs have broad knowledge but can't know your data, and their knowledge becomes stale after training. RAG injects relevant, up-to-date context into the prompt at inference time.

Why RAG?

Instead of fine-tuning a model on your proprietary data (expensive, slow, still hallucinates), you retrieve the most relevant chunks from your knowledge base and inject them into the prompt. The model then generates an answer grounded in that retrieved context.

Analogy: The Open-Book Exam

Fine-tuning = studying for weeks, hoping you memorize everything. RAG = bringing your textbooks to the exam and looking up the answer when needed. RAG is cheaper, more controllable, and keeps your data fresh.

Production Reality

Most production systems use both. Fine-tune a smaller model on your domain for cost/latency, then augment it with RAG for factual grounding. For example: fine-tune a 7B model to understand your company's terminology, then use RAG to inject current product specs and pricing.

Real-World RAG Examples

Customer Support Bot: Index your help center articles, product docs, and past ticket resolutions. When a customer asks "How do I reset my API key?", retrieve the relevant doc section and generate a contextual answer with a link to the source.

Legal Contract Review: Index thousands of contracts. Lawyers ask "What are the termination clauses in our agreements with suppliers in Germany?" The system retrieves relevant clauses across multiple contracts and synthesizes the answer.

Internal Knowledge Base: Index Confluence, Notion, Slack, and Google Drive. Engineers ask "What was the decision on the database migration?" and get an answer grounded in meeting notes and design docs.

Vector Embeddings

What Are Embeddings?

An embedding is a dense vector representation of a piece of text (word, sentence, paragraph, or document) in a continuous vector space. Semantically similar texts end up as nearby points in this space. The key insight: meaning is encoded as geometry.

How Embedding Models Work

Modern embedding models (like OpenAI's text-embedding-3-large, Cohere's embed-v3, or open-source BGE-M3) are typically transformer encoders trained with contrastive learning:

Step 1: Take pairs of texts known to be semantically similar (e.g., a question and its answer, a query and a relevant document).

Step 2: Encode both texts through the transformer to produce vectors.

Step 3: Train the model to push similar pairs closer together and pull dissimilar pairs apart in the vector space. This is the contrastive loss (e.g., InfoNCE).

Step 4: The final embedding is typically the mean of all token embeddings (mean pooling) or the [CLS] token's representation.

Similarity Metrics

Metric Formula Range Best For

Cosine Similarity cos(A,B) = A·B / (‖A‖·‖B‖) [-1, 1] Most common; handles varying text lengths well. Measures angle between vectors, ignoring magnitude.

Dot Product A·B = Σ(Aᵢ × Bᵢ) (-∞, +∞) When magnitude matters (e.g., document importance). Faster to compute. Equivalent to cosine if vectors are normalized.

Euclidean (L2) ‖A-B‖ = √Σ(Aᵢ-Bᵢ)² [0, +∞) When absolute distance matters. Less common for text; more for image embeddings.

Practical Tip

Most embedding models produce normalized vectors (unit length), which means cosine similarity and dot product give identical results. Always check your model's documentation — if it normalizes, use dot product for speed.

Key Trade-offs

Dimension count: Higher dimensions = more expressive, but more storage and slower search. 768–1536 is the sweet spot for most applications. OpenAI's Matryoshka-style models let you truncate dimensions at query time.

Asymmetric search: Some models (Cohere, E5) are trained with separate "query" and "document" modes. A short query like "API rate limits" retrieves long document passages. Using the right mode improves recall by 5–15%.

Self-hosting vs API: Open-source models (BGE, Nomic, E5) can run on your own GPU, eliminating API costs and data-privacy concerns. A single A10G GPU can serve 100+ req/sec for a 768-dim model.

Chunking Strategies

Why Chunking Is Necessary

Embedding models have max token limits (512–8192). Even if you could embed an entire document, the embedding would be too "diluted" — a 50-page document compressed into one 1024-dim vector loses most specific details. Chunking lets you create focused, retrievable units of meaning.

The Chunking Paradox

Too small: Chunks lack context → the LLM can't understand them without surrounding information.

Too large: Chunks mix multiple topics → retrieval returns irrelevant content alongside relevant content, diluting precision.

Chunking Methods

1. Fixed-Size Chunking

Split text into chunks of N tokens/characters with an overlap of M tokens.

# Fixed chunking with overlap

def fixed_chunk(text, chunk_size=500, overlap=50):

words = text.split()

chunks = []

for i in range(0, len(words), chunk_size - overlap):

chunk = ' '.join(words[i:i + chunk_size])

chunks.append(chunk)

return chunks

✓ Pros

Simple to implement

Predictable chunk sizes

Fast processing

✗ Cons

Splits mid-sentence / mid-thought

No semantic awareness

Overlap wastes storage

2. Semantic Chunking

Uses sentence embeddings to detect topic boundaries. Compute the cosine similarity between consecutive sentences; when similarity drops below a threshold, split there.

# Semantic chunking (conceptual)

from sentence_transformers import SentenceTransformer

import numpy as np

model = SentenceTransformer('all-MiniLM-L6-v2')

def semantic_chunk(sentences, threshold=0.75):

embeddings = model.encode(sentences)

chunks, current_chunk = [], [sentences[0]]


for i in range(1, len(sentences)):

sim = np.dot(embeddings[i], embeddings[i-1]) / (

np.linalg.norm(embeddings[i]) * np.linalg.norm(embeddings[i-1])

)

if sim < threshold:

chunks.append(' '.join(current_chunk))

current_chunk = [sentences[i]]

else:

current_chunk.append(sentences[i])


chunks.append(' '.join(current_chunk))

return chunks

3. Recursive / Structural Chunking

Respects document structure. Split first by headers (H1 → H2 → H3), then by paragraphs, then by sentences if still too large. This preserves the natural hierarchy of the document.

4. Sliding Window with Stride

Overlapping windows ensure no information is lost at boundaries. A 512-token window with 128-token stride means each point in the document is covered by ~4 chunks.

Chunk Size Trade-offs

Chunk Size Retrieval Precision Context Quality Best For

128–256 tokens High (very specific) Low (needs surrounding context) FAQ, factoid questions, code snippets

256–512 tokens Balanced Balanced Most general-purpose RAG

512–1024 tokens Lower (more noise) High (self-contained) Long-form analysis, legal docs, narratives

Best Practices by Data Type

Data Type Strategy Details

PDFs Structural + fallback to fixed Extract sections via headers/page structure. Use OCR for scanned docs. Preserve tables as markdown or JSON.

Code AST-based chunking Split by function/class boundaries using the AST. Keep docstrings with their functions. Include import context.

HTML DOM-tree aware Chunk by semantic HTML elements (article, section, main). Strip nav/footer. Preserve heading hierarchy.

Chat logs / Slack Thread-based Keep conversation threads intact. Each thread = one chunk. Add metadata: participants, channel, date.

Tables / CSV Row-group or column-description Don't chunk row-by-row. Group related rows. Prepend column headers to each chunk. Consider serializing as natural language.

Advanced: Parent-Child Chunking

Index small chunks for precise retrieval, but when a small chunk matches, return its parent (larger surrounding context) to the LLM. This gives you the best of both worlds: precise matching + rich context.

Vector Search

How Vector Databases Work

A vector database stores embeddings and supports fast approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) queries. When you search for "how to reset my password," your query is embedded into the same vector space, and the database finds the K closest stored vectors.

The naive approach (brute-force comparison against every vector) is O(n×d) — unusable at millions of vectors. ANN algorithms trade a small accuracy loss for massive speed gains.

ANN Algorithms

HNSW (Hierarchical Navigable Small World)

The most popular ANN algorithm. Builds a multi-layer graph where each node is a vector. Higher layers have fewer, more dispersed connections (for coarse navigation), lower layers have dense local connections (for precise search).

HNSW Graph Structure:

  Layer 2 (sparse): A ──────────────────── F

  │ │

Layer 1 (medium): A ──── C ──── E ────── F

  │ │ │ │

Layer 0 (dense): A ─ B ─ C ─ D ─ E ─ F ─ G ─ H

Search: Enter at top layer, greedily navigate to closest node,

drop to next layer, repeat until Layer 0.

Return K nearest neighbors from Layer 0 search.

Build time: O(n·log(n)) Search time: O(log(n))

Memory: O(n·M) where M = connections per node


IVF (Inverted File Index)

Clusters vectors using K-means into nlist cells. At query time, only searches the nprobe nearest cells instead of all vectors. Think of it as a library: instead of searching every shelf, you first determine which section your book is in.

Product Quantization (PQ)

Compresses vectors by splitting each into sub-vectors and quantizing each sub-vector to the nearest centroid from a learned codebook. Reduces memory by 10–50×, with some accuracy loss. Often combined with IVF as IVF-PQ.

Reranking Techniques

Why Reranking?

Vector search is a bi-encoder approach: query and documents are embedded independently, then compared. This is fast but loses nuance — it can't capture fine-grained interactions between query and document tokens.

Reranking adds a second stage that jointly processes the query and each candidate document, capturing deeper semantic relationships. It's like the difference between scanning book covers (retrieval) versus reading the first page of each (reranking).

Pure vector search misses exact matches (product IDs, error codes, names). Pure keyword search (BM25) misses semantic matches. Hybrid search combines both:

Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF) is the most common merging strategy. For each document, sum 1/(k + rank) across all retrieval methods, where k is typically 60. Documents that rank highly in multiple methods get boosted.

Production Configuration

In practice, the strongest pipeline is: Hybrid (BM25 + vector) → Reranker → LLM. Weaviate, Qdrant, and Pinecone all support hybrid search natively. For reranking, Cohere's Rerank API or open-source bge-reranker-v2-m3 are proven production choices.

Structuring Knowledge Bases

Collection Strategy

Don't dump everything into one collection. Organize by access pattern: one collection per product, per language, or per document type. Use metadata filtering within collections for finer granularity.

Hierarchical Indexing

For large knowledge bases, use a two-level approach: first, a "summary index" with document-level embeddings to identify which documents are relevant, then a "chunk index" within those documents for precise retrieval. This dramatically reduces the search space.

Incremental Indexing and Updates

Documents change. You need a strategy for keeping your index fresh without re-embedding everything.

Content hashing: Hash each chunk's text. On re-index, only re-embed chunks whose hash has changed. This avoids redundant API calls.

Upsert pattern: Use deterministic chunk IDs (e.g., sha256(source_path + chunk_index)). Upsert replaces existing vectors with the same ID, keeping your index clean.

Soft delete + TTL: Mark stale chunks as deleted (metadata flag) rather than immediately removing them. Periodically garbage-collect. This prevents serving stale results during re-indexing.

Change detection: For web sources, use Last-Modified or ETag headers. For file systems, use mtime. For databases, use updated_at timestamps. For APIs, use webhooks.

Latency Budget

A typical RAG request should complete in under 3 seconds for a good user experience. Here's how the budget typically breaks down:

Stage Target Latency Optimization

Query embedding 20–50ms Batch queries, use smaller embedding model for queries

Vector search 10–50ms HNSW index, in-memory, metadata pre-filtering

Reranking 100–300ms Limit to top-20 candidates, use lighter reranker for high-QPS

LLM generation 1–3s (streaming) Stream first token in <500ms, use smaller model for simple queries

Total 1.5–3.5s Cache, model routing, async where possible

Caching Strategies

Semantic cache: Hash the query embedding (or a quantized version) and cache the full response. If a semantically identical query comes in, return the cached result. Tools like GPTCache implement this. Be careful with cache invalidation when your knowledge base updates.

Embedding cache: Cache computed embeddings for frequently queried text. Saves embedding API costs.

Chunk cache: After retrieval, cache the (query_hash → retrieved_chunks) mapping. Even if the LLM response isn't cached, you save the retrieval latency on repeated queries.

Cost Optimization

Model routing: Use a small classifier to route simple queries to a cheaper/faster model (e.g., Haiku) and complex queries to a more capable model (e.g., Sonnet or Opus). This can cut costs by 60–80% without meaningful quality loss.

Context packing: Don't blindly stuff all top-K chunks into the prompt. Dynamically select the minimum context needed. Fewer input tokens = lower cost = faster response.

Evaluation Metrics

What to Measure Metric How

Retrieval quality Recall@K, MRR, NDCG Does the correct chunk appear in top-K results?

Answer correctness Factual accuracy (LLM-as-judge) Use a strong LLM to grade answers against ground truth

Answer groundedness Faithfulness score Is the answer supported by the retrieved chunks? (RAGAS framework)

Answer relevance Relevance score Does the answer actually address the user's question?

End-to-end User satisfaction, task completion rate A/B testing, thumbs-up/down, resolution rate

Evaluation is Non-Negotiable

The #1 mistake in production RAG is not building an evaluation pipeline. Without eval, you're flying blind. Build a test set of 50–100 (question, expected_answer, relevant_source) triples. Run automated evals on every change to your chunking, embedding, or retrieval logic. Use frameworks like RAGAS, DeepEval, or Phoenix (Arize).

Minimal RAG Pipeline in Python

# pip install openai chromadb langchain-text-splitters

import openai

import chromadb

from langchain_text_splitters import RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter

# --- 1. Initialize ---

client = openai. OpenAI()

chroma = chromadb. Client()

collection = chroma.create_collection("my_docs")

# --- 2. Chunk documents ---

splitter = RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter(

chunk_size=500,

chunk_overlap=50,

separators=["\n\n", "\n", ". ", " "]

)

documents = ["Your document text here..."] # Load from files

chunks = []

for doc in documents:

chunks.extend(splitter.split_text(doc))

# --- 3. Embed and store ---

for i, chunk in enumerate(chunks):

response = client.embeddings.create(

model="text-embedding-3-small",

input=chunk

)

collection.add(

ids=[f"chunk_{i}"],

embeddings=[response.data[0].embedding],

documents=[chunk],

metadatas=[{"source": "doc1", "chunk_index": i}]

)

# --- 4. Query ---

def rag_query(question: str, top_k: int = 5) -> str:

# Embed the question

q_emb = client.embeddings.create(

model="text-embedding-3-small",

input=question

).data[0].embedding


# Retrieve relevant chunks

results = collection.query(

query_embeddings=[q_emb],

n_results=top_k

)


context = "\n\n---\n\n".join(results["documents"][0])


# Generate answer

response = client.chat.completions.create(

model="claude-sonnet-4-20250514", # or gpt-4o

messages=[

{"role": "system", "content": (

"Answer based on the provided context. "

"If the context doesn't contain the answer, say so. "

"Cite the relevant section."

)},

{"role": "user", "content": (

f"Context:\n{context}\n\n"

f"Question: {question}"

)}

]

)


return response.choices[0].message.content

# --- 5. Use it ---

answer = rag_query("How do I reset my API key?")

print(answer)

// npm install openai chromadb

import OpenAI from 'openai';

import { ChromaClient } from 'chromadb';

const openai = new OpenAI();

const chroma = new ChromaClient();

async function buildIndex(chunks) {

const collection = await chroma.getOrCreateCollection({ name: 'my_docs' });


// Embed in batches

const batchSize = 100;

for (let i = 0; i < chunks.length; i += batchSize) {

const batch = chunks.slice(i, i + batchSize);

const response = await openai.embeddings.create({

model: 'text-embedding-3-small',

input: batch

});


await collection.add({

ids: batch.map((_, j) => `chunk_${i + j}`),

embeddings: response.data.map(d => d.embedding),

documents: batch

});

}

return collection;

}

async function ragQuery(collection, question) {

const qEmb = await openai.embeddings.create({

model: 'text-embedding-3-small',

input: question

});


const results = await collection.query({

queryEmbeddings: [qEmb.data[0].embedding],

nResults: 5

});


const context = results.documents[0].join('\n\n---\n\n');


const response = await openai.chat.completions.create({

model: 'gpt-4o',

messages: [

{ role: 'system', content: 'Answer based on the provided context. Cite sources.' },

{ role: 'user', content: `Context:\n${context}\n\nQuestion: ${question}` }

]

});


return response.choices[0].message.content;

}

Common Mistakes & Resources

Mistakes to Avoid

# Mistake Why It Hurts What to Do Instead

1 No evaluation pipeline You can't improve what you can't measure Build a test set from day one. Automate eval on every change.

2 One-size-fits-all chunks PDFs, code, and chat logs need different strategies Match chunking strategy to document type. Test different sizes.

3 Ignoring metadata Vector search alone can't distinguish "API v2" from "API v3" Store rich metadata. Use pre-filtering before vector search.

4 Skipping the reranker Top-K from vector search often includes 30–50% irrelevant results Always add a reranker in production. The quality lift justifies the latency.

5 Stuffing too much context LLMs get confused by irrelevant context ("lost in the middle" problem) Be selective. 3–5 highly relevant chunks beat 20 loosely relevant ones.

6 No observability When quality degrades, you have no idea why Log queries, retrieved chunks, LLM inputs/outputs. Trace end-to-end.

7 Starting with a framework LangChain/LlamaIndex abstract away understanding Build your first RAG pipeline from scratch. Use frameworks later for productivity.

8 Wrong embedding model for the job Using a general-purpose model for code, or a monolingual model for multilingual content Benchmark 2–3 embedding models on your actual data before committing.

9 Ignoring the prompt A poorly written system prompt wastes good retrieval Iterate on your prompt as much as your retrieval pipeline. Include instructions for citing sources and admitting uncertainty.

10 Not handling updates Your index becomes stale and serves wrong answers Build incremental indexing from the start. Use content hashing.
